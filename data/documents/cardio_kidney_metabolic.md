# Cardio-Kidney-Metabolic (CKM) Syndrome

## What is CKM syndrome?

Cardio-kidney-metabolic (CKM) syndrome describes the close, two-way connections
between heart disease, kidney disease, and metabolic conditions such as obesity
and type 2 diabetes. These organs and conditions influence one another, so a
problem in one area often drives problems in the others. Recognizing CKM
syndrome helps care teams treat the whole person rather than one organ at a
time.

## How the connections work

- **Metabolic conditions affect the heart and kidneys.** Excess weight, high
  blood sugar, and high blood pressure damage blood vessels throughout the body,
  including the small vessels of the kidneys and the heart muscle.
- **Kidney disease affects the heart.** When kidneys cannot remove enough fluid
  and waste, blood pressure rises and fluid builds up, putting extra strain on
  the heart. This is a common driver of HFpEF.
- **Heart failure affects the kidneys.** A weaker or stiffer heart delivers less
  blood flow to the kidneys, which can worsen kidney function over time.

This cycle means that HFpEF, chronic kidney disease, diabetes, and obesity
frequently appear together.

## Why a combined approach helps

Treating CKM syndrome as a connected whole can protect several organs at once.
For example:

- **SGLT2 inhibitors** can protect both the heart and the kidneys and help
  manage blood sugar.
- **Blood pressure control** reduces strain on the heart and slows kidney
  damage.
- **Weight management and physical activity** improve blood sugar, blood
  pressure, and heart-failure symptoms together.
- **GLP-1 receptor agonists** may help people with obesity and type 2 diabetes
  reduce weight and cardiovascular risk.

## What patients can do

- Keep regular appointments so blood pressure, blood sugar, kidney function, and
  weight can be tracked over time.
- Ask whether your medicines protect more than one organ system.
- Tell every member of your care team about all of your conditions and
  medicines, since heart, kidney, and metabolic care are connected.
- Report swelling, sudden weight gain, or changes in how much you are urinating,
  as these can be early signs of fluid or kidney trouble.

## Monitoring

People with CKM syndrome usually have regular blood and urine tests to watch
kidney function (such as creatinine and eGFR), blood sugar (such as HbA1c),
cholesterol, and electrolytes like potassium. These tests guide safe medicine
choices and dose adjustments.
