# Understanding Heart Failure with Preserved Ejection Fraction (HFpEF)

## What is HFpEF?

Heart failure with preserved ejection fraction (HFpEF) is a type of heart
failure in which the heart's main pumping chamber (the left ventricle) squeezes
normally, but the muscle has become stiff and does not relax properly between
beats. Because the chamber cannot fill with enough blood, the heart pumps out
less blood than the body needs, even though the ejection fraction (the
percentage of blood pushed out with each beat) looks normal, usually 50% or
higher.

HFpEF is sometimes called "diastolic heart failure" because the problem happens
during diastole, the phase when the heart relaxes and fills.

## Who gets HFpEF?

HFpEF becomes more common with age and is more frequent in women. It is strongly
linked to other long-term conditions, including high blood pressure
(hypertension), obesity, type 2 diabetes, chronic kidney disease, and atrial
fibrillation. About half of all people living with heart failure have the
preserved ejection fraction type.

## Common symptoms

People with HFpEF often notice:

- Shortness of breath, especially during activity or when lying flat
- Fatigue and reduced ability to exercise
- Swelling (edema) in the legs, ankles, or abdomen
- Rapid weight gain from fluid buildup
- Waking up at night feeling breathless

These symptoms happen because fluid backs up into the lungs and body when the
stiff heart cannot keep up with demand.

## How HFpEF is diagnosed

Diagnosis usually combines a physical exam, blood tests such as natriuretic
peptides (BNP or NT-proBNP), and an echocardiogram (an ultrasound of the heart)
that shows a normal ejection fraction together with signs of a stiff, poorly
relaxing ventricle. Doctors also look for the underlying conditions that drive
HFpEF, such as high blood pressure and diabetes.

## Why it matters

HFpEF is a long-term condition that can reduce quality of life and lead to
repeated hospital stays if it is not managed. The good news is that controlling
blood pressure, managing weight and diabetes, staying active, and taking
prescribed medicines can help many people feel better and stay out of the
hospital.
