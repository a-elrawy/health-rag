# Treatment Options for HFpEF

There is no single cure for heart failure with preserved ejection fraction
(HFpEF), but a combination of medicines, treatment of related conditions, and
lifestyle changes can reduce symptoms, lower the risk of hospital stays, and
improve quality of life. Treatment is always tailored to the individual by their
healthcare team.

## Medicines commonly used

### SGLT2 inhibitors
Sodium-glucose co-transporter-2 (SGLT2) inhibitors, such as dapagliflozin and
empagliflozin, are now a cornerstone of HFpEF treatment. Large clinical trials
showed they reduce hospitalizations for heart failure across the range of
ejection fraction, including HFpEF. They were first developed for diabetes but
help people with and without diabetes.

### Diuretics (water pills)
Diuretics such as furosemide help the body remove extra fluid. They ease
shortness of breath and swelling, but they treat symptoms rather than the
underlying stiffness. Doses are adjusted based on weight, symptoms, and kidney
function.

### Blood pressure medicines
Because high blood pressure is a major driver of HFpEF, good blood pressure
control is essential. Medicines may include ACE inhibitors, angiotensin receptor
blockers (ARBs), the combination drug sacubitril/valsartan, or
mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists (MRAs) such as spironolactone. The choice
depends on the person's blood pressure, kidney function, and potassium levels.

### Managing heart rhythm
Many people with HFpEF also have atrial fibrillation. Controlling the heart rate
and rhythm, and using blood thinners when appropriate, is an important part of
care.

## Treating the conditions behind HFpEF

Because HFpEF rarely travels alone, treatment focuses heavily on the conditions
that cause and worsen it: high blood pressure, type 2 diabetes, obesity, chronic
kidney disease, coronary artery disease, and sleep apnea. Treating these
conditions well often does more for symptoms than any single heart-failure pill.

## Questions to ask your doctor

- Which of my medicines are treating the heart failure itself, and which are
  treating my blood pressure, diabetes, or kidneys?
- Would an SGLT2 inhibitor be appropriate for me?
- How should I adjust my water pill if my weight or swelling changes?
- What blood tests do I need to monitor my kidneys and potassium?
- What weight gain or symptom changes should prompt me to call you?

## The role of cardiac rehabilitation

Supervised exercise programs (cardiac rehabilitation) can safely improve
exercise tolerance and quality of life for many people with HFpEF. Ask your
care team whether a structured program is right for you.
